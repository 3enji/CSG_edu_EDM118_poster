1. What is Cultural Capital?

Non-financial social assets that promote social mobility beyond economic means (Bourdieu, 1984; Davies and Rizk, 2018)

This encompasses knowledge, skills, education, and any advantages a person has that give them a higher status in society.

Literature snapshot

Key findings from Hockings et al. (2007), investigating the experience of socially-disadvantaged students, found that such students:

  1. Have a tendency to work while in HE to support themselves independently.
  2. May experience difficulty in mixing socially, resulting in social isolation that can impact confidence and commitment to HE courses - social skills are required to navigate this.
  3. May aspire to establish a different identity from their family background, the cost of which may be alienating former peer networks.
  4. Expect fair treatment and not to be patronised, while recognising that differential treatment might be aimed at meeting individual needs rather than singling out students.

3. Relevance for personal tutors (PTs) and institutions

Cultural capital has become more of an issue in the modern HE context where widening participation has led to an increase in student numbers from areas with historically low participation rates.

Deficits of cultural capital may affect whether potential students apply to university in the first place, which institutions they apply to and what they understand before beginning (the first two points are beyond the PT's control).

Social disadvantage may impact behaviour, attendance and course completion. Low attainment is linked to social disadvantage.

Universities charging the highest-level tuition fees are obliged to ensure students are not disadvantaged and must implement an Office for Students' Access and Participation Plan.

Teachers who understand diverse backgrounds are more likely to 'create learning environments and teaching strategies' that suit all (Hockings et al., 2007).

Identifying at-risk students

Factors for PTs to consider that might combine to place a student at risk of course withdrawal or failure (Lochtie et al., 2018):

Student characteristics: a history of alcohol &/or substance abuse, disability, mental health issues, part-time, mature, previous offender

Home life: abusive relationships, care leaver, homelessness, pregnant &/or parent, commuter &/or lives with parent

Cultural/economic factors: socioeconomic groups D/E, ethnic minority, asylum seeker, international

4. The vital role of personal tutors in student success

Direct Student Interaction: PTs have regular contact with students, allowing them to build meaningful relationships and provide tailored guidance.

Holistic Understanding: the role of PT provides a broader picture of students' academic and personal challenges, ensuring a well-rounded support system.

Trust and Approachability: through sustained interactions, PTs can develop trust, making students more likely to seek help when needed (Fukuyama, 1995; Putnam, 2000).

Institutional Knowledge: PTs understand HE structures and services, making them well equipped to signpost students to relevant resources and support networks.

Academic and Non-Cognitive Skill Development: PTs can provide structured guidance on study strategies, time management, and professional networking, enhancing students' long-term success (Dweck et al., 2014; Farrington et al., 2012; Luthar et al., 2015).
