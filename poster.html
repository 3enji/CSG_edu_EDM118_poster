1. What is Cultural Capital?

'Non-financial social assets that promote social mobility beyond economic means' - Bourdieu & Davies, S., & Rizk, J. (2018)

This encompasses knowledge, skills, education, and any advantages a person has that give them a higher status in society.

1. Embodied

  • Academic language
  • Confidence

2. Institutionalised

  • Institutional reputation
  • Professional accreditation

3. Objectified

  • Technology & Equipment
  • Luxury goods

Educational attainment and a range of factors in areas of social deprivation are highly correlated: local economic activity rate, percentage of the population claiming benefits and/or in social housing, health scores, teenage pregnancy rates, etc. (Smith, 2012)

2. Relevance for individuals and institutions

Many students from disadvantaged backgrounds lack the knowledge and ability that students from relatively advantaged backgrounds possess, facilitating success at university and allowing for the further accumulation of cultural capital while experiencing HE—first characterised as cultural capital by Bourdieu (1979).

More of an issue in the context of modern higher education due to widening participation and an increase in the number of 'Non-traditional' students from areas with historically low participation rates.

Deficits of cultural capital may affect young people applying to university in the first place, which institutions they apply to, and what they understand before beginning.

Social disadvantage may impact behaviour, attendance, and course completion. Low attainment is linked to social disadvantage.

Universities are obliged to ensure students are not disadvantaged and must implement an Office for Students' Access and Participation Plan if they charge the highest-level tuition fees.

Teachers who understand diverse backgrounds are more likely to 'create learning environments and teaching strategies' that suit all. (Hockings et al. (2007))

Risk Factors for Course Failure or Withdrawal

Lochtie et al (2019) categorise a series of risk factors, the interplay between which may place a student at risk of course failure or withdrawal:

  1. Student characteristics: a history of alcohol/substance abuse, disability, mental health, part-time, mature, previous offender
  2. Home life: abusive relationships, care leaver, homelessness, pregnant/parent, commuter/lives with parent
  3. Cultural/economic factors: socioeconomic groups D/E, ethnic minority, asylum seeker, international

3. Why personal tutors?

Direct Student Interaction: Personal tutors have regular contact with students, allowing them to build meaningful relationships and provide tailored guidance.

Holistic Understanding: Their role enables them to see the broader picture of a student's academic and personal challenges, ensuring a well-rounded support system.

Trust and Approachability: Through sustained interactions, tutors can develop trust, making students more likely to seek help when needed (Fukuyama, 1995; Putnam, 2000).

Institutional Knowledge: Tutors understand university structures and services, making them well-equipped to signpost students to relevant resources and support networks (Simon et al., 2021).

Academic and Non-Cognitive Skill Development: They can provide structured guidance on study strategies, time management, and professional networking, enhancing students' long-term success (Dweck et al., 2014; Farrington et al., 2012; Luthar et al., 2015).

Evidence base?

Key findings from a study by Hockings et al (2007) into the experience of non-traditional students:

  1. A tendency to work while at university to support themselves independently and ramifications of this.
  2. Sometimes experience difficulty in mixing socially, which results in social isolation and can impact 'confidence and commitment to their course' - social skills are required to navigate this.
  3. Establishing a different identity from family background was seen as an aspiration for some but tended to alienate former peers.
  4. Expected fair treatment, not to be patronised, while recognising that differential treatment might be meeting individual needs rather than singling out.
